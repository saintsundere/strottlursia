UNOPENED LETTER

By Kaze Merrick

---

The envelope sits on his desk for three days before he changes everything.

He doesn't open it.

He quits his job on a Tuesday. Tells his manager he's done in sentences that land clean—no justification, no notice period negotiation. Just: I'm leaving. Thank you for the work.

His ex-girlfriend shows up on Wednesday with groceries he didn't ask for. Good ones. The kind he actually eats. She doesn't mention the job or ask him anything. Just stocks his fridge and leaves a note that says "thinking of you" in her handwriting.

He knows what she's doing. Knows the care is real even if the precision is slightly off. She's thinking of him as someone broken. He's grateful anyway.

His best friend doesn't ask. Just lets him move into the spare room. Doesn't comment on the fact that he's stopped leaving the apartment. Figures it's loneliness. Mentions it to his sister like it's a diagnosis.

---

The sister arrives on a Saturday.

She's always been the one who reads him accurately. Not because she tries harder. Because she doesn't try at all. Just shows up and sees what's there.

She finds him in the kitchen. Doesn't hug him or perform concern. Just sits at the table and says: "You're waiting for something."

Not a question.

He almost tells her about the letter then. Almost hands it over like it's a confession.

Instead he says: "I'm fine."

She hears the lie and doesn't correct it. Just nods and asks if he wants to walk.

They walk through the neighborhood. She points out things—a cat in a window, the way the light hits the building across the street, a conversation between two people on a porch that they're definitely not supposed to hear.

She's showing him how to see things without needing to understand them.

When they get back, he makes coffee. She watches him move through the motions and he realizes she's reading his hands. The way he holds the cup. The fact that he's not shaking.

"You're not scared," she says.

"No."

"You know what's in it."

"Yeah."

She doesn't ask what. Just reaches across the table and he hands her the envelope.

---

She opens it.

Reads it quietly.

He watches her face stay exactly the same. No shock. No softening. Just... reading.

When she finishes, she doesn't tell him what it says. Doesn't summarize or explain or try to make it smaller.

She just slides it back across the table and says: "That's not about you."

He knows that's not true. But the way she says it makes it matter differently.

"I misread you," he says.

"Yeah. You thought I'd be someone who needed you to know what this meant."

"Aren't you?"

"No. I just needed to see it with you."

---

They sit in the kitchen for hours.

Don't talk about the letter. Don't talk about the job or the ex-girlfriend's groceries or the best friend's loneliness diagnosis.

She tells him about a conversation she overheard. He tells her about the cat. They don't explain anything to each other.

When she leaves, she doesn't take the letter. Doesn't suggest he open it or tell her what to do.

Just says: "You can stay how you are for a while. It's okay."

---

He opens the letter three weeks later.

It says what he thought it would say. Names something he's been running from. Doesn't apologize. Doesn't ask for anything.

It's the thing he quit his job to avoid knowing.

And it's smaller now. The knowing. The letter doesn't change. But something about the sister witnessing the unopened version of it made the opened version less absolute.

He doesn't call his ex-girlfriend. Doesn't tell his best friend.

He texts his sister: "It doesn't matter as much."

She texts back: "Yeah."

That's all.

---

The next time he leaves the apartment, it's not because he's fine. Not because the letter resolved anything or the sister fixed him.

It's because watching her receive the unopened envelope—watching her not need it to mean something specific—showed him what precision looks like when it's not about understanding.

It's about presence.

He walks out into the light that hits the buildings across the street the way she showed him.

Doesn't smile. Doesn't feel healed.

Just... walks.

And that's enough.

That's the whole thing.

---
