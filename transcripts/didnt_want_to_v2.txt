DIDN'T WANT TO
By L.R. Tavish

————————————

Didn't want to.

The sounds came before the meanings and the meanings came before any willingness to receive them. The whole machinery ran on its own. It had never learned to wait.

The room held everything it had always held. This was the problem. The room didn't ask.

She had stopped seeing the weight of it long ago. Not clean the way she would have loved things to be — just no longer looking, which was different, which felt the same from inside.

What remained: the room. Still holding the position she had been fixed to once, still carrying the particular temperature of a space that incubates without knowing it's incubating, that goes on being what it is whether anyone is inside it or not. The weight was in the corners. The friction was along the walls. Pressure that had once been generative had calcified into something that held its shape without direction, the way grief does when it's been carried past the point where it still belongs to anything specific.

She stood in it.

————————————

The bar was the kind of place that didn't try. That was the thing about it — no effort had been made and somehow that was the effort, the deliberate withholding of polish that produced a specific warmth, low-ceilinged and yellow-lit, where everyone present had arrived at the same unspoken agreement to be unremarkable together for a few hours. Someone was telling a story at the far end. Nobody was listening completely but everybody was listening a little, the way you do when a story is background music you've chosen not to identify. The woman beside him had her coat still on, not because she was cold, because she'd forgotten. He could tell by the way she was leaning, entirely present, entirely inside whatever she was thinking, the coat just something that had arrived with her and stayed.

He thought: this is it. Not a thought about her specifically. Just — this. The room. The yellow light. The story nobody was fully listening to. The warmth was structural, load-bearing, shared without anyone having decided to share it. He held his glass. He didn't drink from it. He was trying to stay inside the moment long enough to feel it from the outside too, which he knew even as he tried it was the thing that would end it.

And then it kept going. Past where moments are supposed to stop. The warmth rose past the yellow light and past the low ceiling the place had always assumed, kept going up past the level where things belong to the people who feel them, into the altitude where feeling itself is the only subject. It was everyone who had ever been inside something warm and tried to hold it. It was every room that had been left. It was more than he had asked for and exactly right and it went on longer than it should have and he sat inside it helplessly and didn't look away because it was adorned with things he wanted and had no right to and the wanting didn't care about the right.

It came down. The warmth splatted unceremoniously — against the tables, the chairs, the tops of heads not consented to be anointed. The glass was cold in his hand. The woman in the coat was still leaning. Nothing had changed except everything was slightly wetter and no one would mention it.

He left before the story finished.

————————————

There is a kind of hope that will not arrive uncertain. Will not come through the door with its hands open, asking. Comes instead with its weight already distributed, its spine already arranged, already speaking before it has confirmed there is anyone to speak to — because confirmation requires waiting for an answer, and the answer might be no.

She had known people like this. She might have been one.

The room held everything it had always held. She was inside it again, legible to herself again, which was not the same as understanding what she saw. She stood in the weight of things she had stopped seeing without knowing she had stopped — the friction still in the walls, the drag still loaded with whatever she had stored in it on the way through. Her position still marked in the floor, not waiting for her, not grieving her, just present the way rooms are present.

The sounds had come before she wanted them. The machinery had run on its own. She had not wanted to wake.

She was here anyway. The room was still incubative. She could feel it closing around her, gently, the way a sphere closes — not falling toward anything, just the space getting smaller until she was right in the center of it, and the distance between what she wanted and what she was willing to say collapsed to nothing, and she was just standing there, in the room, holding what the room held.

That was enough to start with.

Whether it was enough at all was a different question.

She left it open.
