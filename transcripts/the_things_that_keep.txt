THE THINGS THAT KEEP
By L.R. Tavish

————————————

The boxes had been in the hall closet for eleven months.

She had told herself, when she moved them there from the bedroom, that this was temporary — a staging area, not a decision. The closet was deep and had a high shelf and once the boxes were up on it she could close the door and the closet looked like any other closet, which was the point. Not hiding. Just not having to see them every time she walked through the room.

The eleven months were not temporary. She understood this now that she was opening the closet with the specific intention of opening it, rather than the habitual daily non-opening that had become its own kind of ritual. She had decided, this morning, for reasons that had not announced themselves as decisions until she was already in the hall with her hands on the door, that today was the day.

She wasn't sure what today was the day for. Getting through them. Distributing them. Deciding what kept and what went. The language of the task shifted depending on the hour and she had learned not to pin it down too specifically.

She carried the first box to the table.

————————————

His name had been Gerald, which he had disliked so thoroughly that by the time she met him, at thirty-four, in a city they had each moved to from smaller cities, the name had become a private joke he told on himself before anyone else could. He introduced himself as Gerry-with-a-G, the G being unnecessary information she'd pointed out on their second date, at which point he'd said the unnecessary information was the whole point of it, that if you told people something useless right away you found out immediately whether they were worth talking to.

She had thought about this for several weeks before she told him she'd passed the test.

He'd said he knew.

————————————

The box contained: a collection of transit maps from cities he had visited and several he had not. A coffee mug she didn't recognize from a diner that had probably closed. Three notebooks, the kind with the black covers, filled in his handwriting that slanted consistently to the right in a way that looked like urgency and turned out, when she'd asked once, to be the result of a brief period in adolescence where he'd decided to change his handwriting and gotten partway through before losing interest. Several pens. A paperback with a receipt used as a bookmark, the receipt from a grocery store in a city she had never visited, dated four years before she knew him.

She held the receipt for a while.

The grocery store receipt. He had been in a city she'd never visited, in a year before she existed to him, buying something for dinner — the receipt said bread, cheese, two cans of something, a bar of soap. Ordinary things. He had been a person buying soap in a city before they'd met, before the introduction at the party where she'd been standing in the wrong corner talking to someone she'd already forgotten, before the first date and the second date and the diner and the mug she didn't recognize and the notebooks slanting to the right.

She set the receipt on the table beside the mug.

————————————

The thing about objects, she had been thinking since the first month when she could still think about it clearly, was that they had no idea what they were supposed to mean. The receipt from the grocery store had been a receipt from a grocery store. It had not been a document of the person's aliveness, of the fact that he had been alive in a city with cheese and soap and nothing as yet decided about who he would eventually be to her. It had not known it would end up in a black-covered notebook in a closet for eleven months while she figured out how to open the door.

Objects were innocent of their significance. This was the hardest part.

She had thought, in the first months, that she wanted things that had been important to him. Things he had chosen with intention. The maps, some of which were framed and had hung on his wall, seemed like the right category of thing — deliberate, representative, evidence of who he'd been. But then she found the receipt and understood that the receipt was what she actually needed. The accidental thing. The thing that had not been selected for meaning but had accumulated it anyway, invisibly, without either of them knowing, over the years of being carried in a notebook through the moves and the reorganizations and the various closets.

He had bought bread and cheese and soap in a city she'd never visited. That was what remained and it was also completely sufficient.

————————————

She went through the notebooks carefully.

He had been, she had known, a person who wrote things down. Not a diary — nothing narrative, nothing that told the story of a day or an event. Lists, mostly. Things to do, things to read, things he didn't want to forget. Phrases that had caught him. The occasional sketch of something he'd seen, done with the unselfconscious line of someone who knew they weren't an artist and had decided the knowing was freeing rather than limiting.

She found a page that said, near the top in fresh ink, the kind that hadn't dried fully and had left a slight impression on the page beneath: things worth keeping.

Under it: the list went down half the page.

She did not read the list. She turned the page and kept going and then she came back to it and read it the second time more slowly, the way you read something the second time when the first time was too fast for what the thing was.

The list was: a good knife, his grandmother's table (crossed out, then re-added with a small asterisk), the habit of reading in the morning before anything else, the specific smell of coffee in a place you've been for a long time, knowing your own unreasonable preferences and honoring them, the inability to pass a bookstore without going in, the willingness to say I don't know when you don't know, two or three conversations you can return to, the knowledge that you are most yourself when you are not trying to be.

She sat with the list for a while.

————————————

She had her own list. She had not written it down, but she knew it in the way you know things you've assembled from the wreckage of attention. The smell of the deli below the apartment she'd lived in for her twenties, which had closed while she was living there and which she had mourned with a specificity that had surprised her. The feeling of being in a library before it opens, in that particular hush that belongs to places that are waiting to be used. Handwriting. The handwriting of people she loved, the specific way their letters were formed, which she had always felt told you more about a person than anything they said in the letters themselves.

She was in the notebook because of the handwriting. The slant that looked like urgency and was really just a project abandoned halfway through. The way the letters pressed slightly harder at the beginning of a word than at the end, which she had never noticed while he was alive and had not been able to stop noticing since.

She turned through the rest of the notebook page by page.

————————————

Near the back she found a page that was blank except for a name.

Her name. Just that. Printed clearly in the center of the page, in the careful hand he used when he wanted something to be legible rather than fast. He'd dated it in the corner — two years before he died, three years after they'd met.

She looked at it for a long time.

She had no way to know what the page was for. Whether it was a list that had only ever been one item, or a draft of something, or a note he'd started and then lost the thread of, or simply the act of writing a name down to see how it looked on a page, the way you sometimes need to make something physical to confirm it's real. She did not know. She would not know. The page offered nothing except the name, legible and centered and dated, which was either a great deal or very little depending on what you decided to do with it.

She decided to do nothing with it.

She closed the notebook.

————————————

Outside, it was the kind of afternoon that arrives in the late part of the year without drama — the light coming at an angle through windows it had been coming through at a different angle for months, the shift so gradual you notice it only when you stop to look. She had not stopped to look in some time.

She went to the window. The street below was the usual street: a woman walking a dog with the efficient stride of someone who had somewhere to be after this. A delivery truck double-parked with its hazards on, the particular orange blink of something that needed a few minutes and knew it. A man she recognized from the building next door sitting on the stoop with a coffee, doing what she had sometimes done in other apartments in other years — using the street as a kind of company, quiet and at a remove, something happening that didn't require anything from you.

She had been good at that once. Sitting in the middle of the ordinary and finding it sufficient.

She thought she might be getting back to it.

————————————

The box took three hours. She worked slowly, which she had not planned and did not try to correct. She held each thing long enough to understand what it was and then she made a decision, and the decisions were not as hard as she had expected, which she suspected was the result of the eleven months of not deciding having done some preparatory work she hadn't known she was doing.

What kept: the maps. The notebooks. The mug she didn't recognize. The receipt from the grocery store in the city she'd never visited. A photograph she found tucked in the back of the last notebook, of a street somewhere, no people, taken at an angle that suggested someone walking rather than stopping, the image slightly blurred at the edges in the way of photographs taken quickly, not because the moment was fleeting but because the person taking it had been in motion and had not wanted to stop.

She looked at the photograph for a long time. She had no idea what street it was. She had no idea which city. He had been in motion somewhere, photographing a street in passing, and the photograph had ended up in a notebook that had ended up in a closet that she had finally opened, and the street was still there somewhere, in its city, with its buildings and its particular light, unaware of any of this.

She put the photograph with the things that kept.

————————————

She carried the box back to the closet when she was done. Not the high shelf — she put it lower, where she could reach it without thinking too hard about it, where it wasn't exactly present but wasn't hidden either.

She closed the closet door.

She stood in the hall for a moment. The apartment held the quiet of the late afternoon, the specific silence that collects in rooms when the light changes and the day begins to reckon with itself. She had been here before, in this particular stillness, and she had learned that it was not emptiness. It was the room after something. The impression, as he would have said.

She went to the kitchen. She put the kettle on. She looked out the window at the street, which was still the usual street, the woman and the dog gone now and the truck gone and the man from the building next door gone inside, the stoop empty, the street itself still there doing what streets do in the interval between the things that happen in them.

The kettle started its low sound.

She thought: next spring she'd go to the city with the grocery store. She didn't know why and didn't press it. She'd book something cheap and go for a few days and walk around and eat at a diner and buy bread and cheese and maybe soap, and she'd see what a city looked like that he had been in before she existed to him, which was to say she'd see what the city looked like, and that would be enough.

She made her tea.

She sat at the table where the things that kept were still arranged, not yet put away, the maps and the notebooks and the mug and the receipt and the photograph of the unknown street.

She sat with them for a while.

They didn't require anything from her.

That was the point of them.
