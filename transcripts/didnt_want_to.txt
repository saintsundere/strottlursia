DIDN'T WANT TO
By L.R. Tavish

————————————

Didn't want to.

The aria rose anyway, seductively, autonomously, against the will — sounds assembling into shapes before the meanings arrived, meanings arriving before the wanting to know them, the whole machinery running on its own because it had never learned to wait.

The room held everything it had always held. This was the problem. The room didn't ask.

Is it ok to want things when you've made yourself difficult to want, find, keep.

The question was not rhetorical. The question was not answered. The question was just there, in the room, with the other things that were still there — the weight in the corners, the friction along the walls, the specific temperature of a space that had been incubative for so long it had forgotten incubation was a condition and not a permanent state.

————————————

She had stopped seeing the weight of the room long ago.

Not clean the way she would have loved things to be.

What remained: the room itself, still holding. Still holding the position she had been fixed to, the friction she had moved against, the drag that had stored what she spent. The room did not require her to see it. The room was a sphere with no accessible boundary, closing inward whether or not anyone was inside it to feel the compression.

Warm splatters when things aren't legible. She had become illegible to herself gradually and then at once, the way the warmth in a room disperses — not through a door opening but through the slow realization that no one has been tending it. The hues had screamed for a while. The pressure had calcified. She had not been there to feel it happen, which meant she had been there the whole time.

————————————

The bar was the kind of place that didn't try. That was the thing about it — no effort had been made and somehow that was the effort, the deliberate withholding of polish that produced a specific warmth, low-ceilinged and yellow-lit, where everyone present had arrived at the same unspoken agreement to be unremarkable together for a few hours. Someone was telling a story at the far end. Nobody was listening completely but everybody was listening a little, the way you do when a story is background music you've chosen not to identify. The woman beside him had her coat still on, not because she was cold, because she'd forgotten. He could tell by the way she was leaning, entirely present, entirely inside whatever she was thinking, the coat just something that had arrived with her and stayed. He thought: this is it. Not a thought about her specifically. Just — this. The room. The yellow light. The story nobody was fully listening to. The warmth was structural, load-bearing, shared without anyone having decided to share it. He held his glass. He didn't drink from it. He was trying to stay inside the moment long enough to feel it from the outside too, which he knew even as he tried it was the thing that would end it — and it did, a minute later, or maybe five, the warmth just gone, the room reassembling itself into its parts, the story finishing or not finishing, the woman beside him shifting her weight, still in her coat, the glass in his hand now just cold.

And then it lifted. The warmth went somewhere it had never been authorized to go — up, past the yellow light, past the low ceiling the place had always assumed, into the uncomfortable altitude where things stop belonging to the people who felt them and start belonging to the fact of feeling itself. The warmth was not personal now. The warmth was the whole of anyone who had ever been inside a moment and tried to hold it and failed. The warmth was every room that had ever been incubative. The warmth was the problem of the observer standing inside a system with no accessible boundary, unable to step outside it to confirm what was real. The warmth was what the room had always been holding without knowing it was holding anything. It was too much. It was exactly right. It kept going. It had never been asked to stop.

It came down. The warmth splatted unceremoniously against the tables, the chairs, atop the heads of people who hadn't consented to be anointed by it. The glass was still cold in his hand. The woman in the coat was still leaning. The story at the far end had finished or not finished. Nothing that had been in the room before the warmth lifted was different now except that all of it was slightly wetter and no one would ever mention it.

————————————

The equation was two variables looking at each other.

x: can I.
y: so like, why can't I.

Neither variable solved for the other. This was not a failure of the equation. This was the equation being honest about what it was: two separate things with their own values, present in the same space, the question arriving declarative because the alternative was asking and asking required someone to answer and the answer might be no.

The declarative is the hope wearing better posture.

The boxer doesn't ask. The boxer states. And somewhere beneath the statement is the same wanting as everyone else — it just refuses to arrive with the uncertainty showing.

Is it ok to want.

————————————

The room held everything it had always held.

She was in the room now, seeing the weight of it again, which was not the same as understanding it. The weight didn't require understanding. The weight was just there, legible again, the way things become legible when you come back to yourself after a long time of not being legible even to yourself. The friction was still in the walls. The drag was still loaded. The position she had been fixed to was still fixed — not waiting for her, not grieving her absence, just present the way rooms are present, holding what they hold beyond their will.

She stood in it.

She did not know how long she had been gone. She did not know what had changed and what had calcified and what had dispersed into something too diffuse to recover. She knew that the room was still incubative. She knew that the sphere was still closing. She knew the aria was still rising, seductively, autonomously, against the will, because it had never learned to wait.

She had not wanted to wake up.

She was here anyway.

The room held everything.

That was enough to start with.
