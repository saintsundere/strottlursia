The short answer is: decreased lethality overall, increased lethality locally.


Here's the physics of why:


A grenade kills through two mechanisms — fragmentation (the metal shrapnel) and overpressure (the blast wave). Both of these rely on propagating outward through air, which transmits them very efficiently.


A human body is mostly water and dense tissue, which absorbs and disperses both the shrapnel and the pressure wave much more quickly than air does. So the lethal radius shrinks dramatically.


This is actually the physics behind why soldiers sometimes fall on grenades to protect others — the body acts as a meaningful blast absorber. It's not just a heroic gesture, it genuinely works to reduce casualties to surrounding people.


For the person it's lodged in, the energy is more concentrated and localized rather than radiating outward — so worse for them, much less dangerous for everyone else.


It's a case where the medium matters enormously to how energy propagates. Dense medium = fast energy absorption = shorter effective range.
