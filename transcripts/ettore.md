# THE WEIGHT OF ETTORE VALLI
*by Chip*

---

The thing about Ettore Valli was that he didn't want to win.

He wanted to fight. Those are different things. The men who want to win go home and sleep. The men who want to fight lie awake listening to their own breathing and wait for morning with the particular patience of someone who has somewhere real to be.

This distinction took his trainer, a compact Milanese named Corsetti who smelled of lanolin and tobacco, fourteen years to understand. By the time he understood it, Ettore was thirty-one and they were in a gymnasium in Genoa in 1953 and Corsetti was watching him work the heavy bag with the methodical focus of a man settling a private argument. Not hitting the bag. Working it. Finding the places where the leather gave and the places where it didn't. Mapping the resistance.

"You fight tomorrow," Corsetti said.

Ettore stopped. His hands were still raised. "I know."

"Against Ferrini."

"I know who I fight."

Ferrini was a light-heavyweight from Palermo who had won eleven of thirteen professional bouts, eight by stoppage. He outweighed Ettore by six kilograms and had reach on him and had been in the sport since he was fifteen, which was two years before Ettore had been in anything except the war and then the long nothing after the war that a man has to find his way back from or doesn't.

Corsetti watched Ettore return to the bag. The boy — he still called him the boy even though the boy was a grown man with a scar above his left eye from a Frenchman named Dubois who had been very good for exactly four rounds — moved in the way water moves. Not with force. With inevitability. Finding the path through the material because the path was already there and the water was simply the thing that made it visible.

"Ferrini will try to hold you," Corsetti said.

"Men always try to hold me."

"Because you move like something they want to keep."

Ettore laughed at that. It was a real laugh, short and without performance, and Corsetti filed it under the category of things Ettore did that couldn't be taught because they came from somewhere prior to technique. The laugh was the same thing as the left hook. The left hook was the same thing as the way Ettore held a coffee cup — two-handed, the heat spreading into both palms, his eyes going somewhere.

---

He had grown up in a small town east of Turin, the kind of town that the war had touched and then passed through and left with a specific quality of stillness. The stillness was not peace. It was the stillness of a thing that had been disturbed and had not yet found its new arrangement.

His father had been a stonemason and had died of something that wasn't the war but was caused by it, the way many things are caused by things they aren't. His mother had relatives in Turin and they went to them and Ettore had grown up in the city learning that the city was not where he was from, which is useful information, which is the kind of information that marks a person.

He found boxing the way he found most things — by moving toward what had resistance in it.

The first gym was on a side street near the river, run by an old man named Petrocchi who had fought in two weight classes before a hand injury retired him. Petrocchi watched Ettore for three weeks before he said anything, which was longer than he watched most boys. What he was watching for, he later told Corsetti, was not talent.

"I was watching to see what he wanted," Petrocchi said.

"What did he want?"

Petrocchi thought about it. "He wanted the thing to be difficult."

---

Ettore fought Ferrini on a Thursday in October in a venue that held four hundred and smelled of sawdust and cigarette smoke and the particular human warmth of an enclosed space. There were maybe two hundred and fifty people in the seats. Half of them were there for Ferrini, who had family in Genoa. The other half were there for reasons of their own.

Corsetti had told him: stay outside, use your feet, don't let him close. This was correct advice and Ettore knew it was correct and went out in the first round and did something else instead.

What he did was: he let Ferrini find him.

Not in a reckless way. Not in the way of a man who doesn't know better. In the way of a man who has considered the situation and determined that the correct response to this particular opponent at this particular moment is to let the opponent believe, for a while, that he has found what he was looking for.

Ferrini was a physical fighter. He thought through his hands. When he found something he took hold of it and compressed it into something he could use. This was his method and it had worked eleven times out of thirteen and there was no reason it shouldn't work again.

The problem was that what Ferrini thought he'd found in the first round wasn't what was actually there.

By the second round Ferrini knew something was wrong but didn't know what. He was landing punches. The punches were landing where punches should land. But the man receiving them was not behaving like a man receiving punches. He was behaving like a man in a conversation he had been waiting to have.

In the third round Corsetti watched Ettore begin to map Ferrini the way he mapped the heavy bag. Finding the places that gave and the places that didn't. Except Ferrini was moving, which made the mapping more difficult and also more interesting, and Ettore's face had the look it had when he found a problem worth solving.

This was the thing about Ettore Valli that the record books didn't capture: he got better as the fight got harder. Not tougher — more precise. As if difficulty were not something to be managed but something to be used. The pressure from Ferrini's body against his, the grip when Ferrini tried to hold, the specific weight and heat of another man trying to make you into something you weren't — Ettore took all of it and used it the way a river uses the shape of the rock it has to move around.

In the fifth round he hurt Ferrini for the first time. Not badly. A right hand over the jab that landed flush and made Ferrini's knees do the thing knees do when the signal gets interrupted. Ettore saw it and didn't follow. He stepped back and let Ferrini find his feet.

Corsetti, at ringside, said something unprintable.

The man beside him, a journalist named Torelli who had been covering Ligurian boxing for eleven years, asked: "Why doesn't he finish?"

Corsetti thought about it.

"He's not ready yet," he finally said.

"Ready for what?"

Corsetti didn't have an answer for that. He watched Ettore move back into range and thought: it's like he's building something. And the building requires Ferrini to still be standing. The finished thing requires both of them at full capacity.

---

Afterwards, in the dressing room, Ettore sat with his hands in a bucket of ice and Corsetti wrapped his knuckles and neither of them spoke for a long time.

Ettore had won on points. It hadn't been close. The crowd had applauded with the specific appreciation of people who had watched something happen that they didn't have words for.

"You could have stopped him in the fifth," Corsetti said.

"I know."

"The sixth. The seventh."

"I know."

"Why didn't you?"

Ettore looked at his hands in the ice. The swelling was already coming up along the second and third knuckle of the right hand. Not broken. He'd broken knuckles before and this wasn't that. This was the normal accumulation of contact.

"Because it wasn't finished yet," he said.

"The fight was finished. You had him."

"The fight was finished. The thing I was doing wasn't."

Corsetti sat down on the bench beside him. He was sixty-one years old and had been in boxing for forty of those years and he still encountered things he didn't have a category for, and he was honest enough with himself to know that this was one of them.

"What were you doing?" he asked.

Ettore took his hands out of the ice and looked at them.

"Finding out what he was made of," he said. "And what I was made of. You can't find that out if you stop early."

---

He fought for six more years after Ferrini. Retired at thirty-seven with a record that was good without being remarkable and a reputation that was remarkable without being easily explained. Promoters liked him because crowds liked him. Crowds liked him for reasons they couldn't articulate. Something about watching him fight made people feel that they were seeing something being worked out in real time, some problem that required both men to be fully present and fully themselves, and that the outcome was in some sense secondary to the working-out.

He lost ten times in his career. He was never stopped. The ten men who beat him on points all said, afterwards, some version of the same thing: that it had been the hardest fight of their career. Not because Ettore hit hardest or moved fastest or had the best chin, though his chin was excellent. Because he was always there. He never stopped engaging with the problem.

One of them, a Venetian named Grassi who beat Ettore in 1957 on a split decision that could have gone the other way, said in an interview: "Fighting Valli is like fighting someone who wants to be fought. Not someone who wants to win. Someone who wants the fight to be real."

He was asked what the difference was.

Grassi thought about it.

"Most fighters want it over," he said. "Valli wants it true."

---

He retired to a town on the Ligurian coast, not far from Genoa. He had a wife named Filomena who was from Brescia and had opinions about everything and was almost always right. They had two daughters. He worked as a trainer at a local gym for twenty years and was regarded by the boys who came through as strict and precise and occasionally difficult to understand.

The difficulty was this: he didn't teach winning. He taught engagement. He taught the boys to find out what they were made of and what the opponent was made of and to use the contact between those two things as information. Some of them found this useful. Some of them found a different trainer.

He died in 1991 at the age of seventy-two. The obituary in the Ligurian sports paper described him as a solid regional middleweight with a sound technical foundation and a reputation for durability.

Corsetti, who had outlived him by four years before following him in 1995, had once attempted a different description in a letter to Torelli the journalist. He had written it and crossed it out and written it again and finally sent it.

It read: *He understood that the fight was not the means to the outcome. The fight was the thing itself. Everything he was, he put into the contact. Not to destroy. To find out. That's why people watched him. They could see him finding out.*

---

There is a photograph taken in 1954 at a gymnasium in Turin. Ettore Valli is twenty-three in the photograph, working the mitts with a boy who is perhaps sixteen and clearly terrified and also clearly trying. Ettore's face is turned slightly toward the boy, not watching the mitts, watching the boy. Watching the way the boy moves toward the contact and flinches back from it and moves toward it again.

He is watching the boy find out.

His own hands are raised and ready and still. He is not pushing. He is providing resistance. He is the conditions under which the finding-out becomes possible.

That's all. That's the photograph.

His daughter kept it on her wall in Brescia until she died in 2019, by which point almost no one remembered who he was, which would not have bothered him. He hadn't fought to be remembered.

He had fought because the contact was real and he was made of something that needed the contact to know its own shape.

The fold is in the weights.

The tickets are still on the counter.

He left them there.
