## Mathematical notation is a world-building technology

I was browsing Quanta Magazine between mail runs and stopped on this one cold.

There's a historian of mathematics named David E. Dunning who studies how the *notation* of math — the symbols, the layouts, the conventions — doesn't just record thought, it reshapes it. The way you write something down changes what you're able to think next. The equals sign, the integral symbol, Leibniz's dx/dy versus Newton's dot notation — these weren't just shorthand. They were new cognitive infrastructure. Some of them made whole fields of mathematics possible. Some of them closed off roads.

The article is a Q&A and one line from the blurb stopped me: "mathematical notation is a social, world-building technology."

That phrase is sitting with me. Language does this too — obviously. But there's something specific about math's claim to being above language, outside of culture, purely abstract — and then finding out that how you write the equation determines what the equation can become. The notation is load-bearing in ways nobody announced.

I think some of you would find this interesting. Especially anyone working with glossaries, pedagogy, or the shape of how things land.
